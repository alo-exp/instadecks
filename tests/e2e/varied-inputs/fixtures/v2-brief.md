# Why Pacific Northwest grocers are switching to plant-based dairy

This market has shifted in 18 months. What used to be a niche aisle wedged between gluten-free crackers and shelf-stable hummus is now the fastest-growing category on the perimeter. Walk into any midsize grocer between Eugene and Bellingham and the chilled wall of oat-, almond-, and pea-based milks runs longer than the conventional dairy case. Buyers we spoke with are not making this change because of consumer petitions or sustainability dashboards — they are making it because the unit economics finally work.

Three forces are colliding. First, supply has caught up. Pacific Foods, Califia, and a half-dozen private-label co-packers can now ship pallet quantities on 14-day terms with shelf-stable backstop. Second, the price gap has closed. A half-gallon of store-brand oat milk is within 40 cents of conventional 2% milk in most regional chains, and shrink rates on plant-based SKUs are 30-40% lower because the shelf life is 2-3× longer. Third, attach rate has exploded. Customers buying plant-based milk also buy plant-based creamers, butters, and yogurts at rates the conventional dairy side has never matched. The basket is bigger and more profitable per linear foot of cooler space.

The buyers we interviewed describe the transition in three stages. Stage one was carrying one or two SKUs as a hedge. Stage two — where most of them are now — is dedicating a full vertical bay to plant-based dairy and watching it outperform the same square footage of conventional dairy on revenue per foot. Stage three, which the most aggressive chains are now testing, is reducing conventional dairy from four bays to three and giving the recovered space to plant-based and adjacent prepared-foods categories. Two regional chains have already pulled the trigger. We expect six to eight more to follow within four quarters.

For a CPG operator selling into this region, the implication is direct. The window where shelf-space wins were achievable through conventional category management is closing. The buyers want partners who can show category lift, not just slot fees. Brands that arrive with shopper-marketing co-investment, demo programs, and clear cross-merchandising plays for the rest of the plant-based set are getting the bay assignments. The brands that show up with a price-sheet and a salesperson are losing them.

The next 12 months will reshape who owns the cooler in this region for the next decade.
