# Field report: Lagos street-food vendors

A photo essay from three days in Yaba, Surulere, and Mushin markets. Each image documents a single vendor, the dish on offer, and one observation about the economics of the stall.

## 1. Mama Sade — Yaba market, 6:40 AM

![Mama Sade folding moin-moin leaves](placeholder-1.jpg)

She has been at this stall for 22 years. Her moin-moin (steamed bean pudding) sells out by 9:00 AM most weekdays. The leaves come from a supplier she has used since 2003; he raises the price exactly once per year, in November.

## 2. Tunde — Surulere, 7:15 AM

![Tunde grilling suya skewers over a charcoal trough](placeholder-2.jpg)

Tunde grills 400 suya skewers between 6 AM and 11 AM, a second batch from 5 PM to 9 PM. He buys his beef from a wholesaler one neighborhood over and pays in cash daily — credit terms have never been on offer to him.

## 3. Iya Funke — Mushin, 11:50 AM

![Iya Funke ladling jollof rice into takeaway packs](placeholder-3.jpg)

Her jollof rice operation is a four-person affair: she cooks, her daughter serves, her son handles the cash, and a hired hand washes pots. The rice cooks in three rotating 60-liter pots over kerosene burners. The kerosene cost has tripled in two years.

## 4. Chinedu — Yaba, 2:30 PM

![Chinedu deep-frying puff-puff in a wok](placeholder-4.jpg)

Puff-puff is the after-school economy. Chinedu sells from 2 PM to 6 PM, exclusively to schoolchildren and their guardians. He raised his price from ₦50 to ₦100 per piece this year. Volume dropped about 12%, revenue rose about 70%.

## 5. Aunty Bola — Surulere, 8:50 PM

![Aunty Bola serving pepper soup at a roadside table](placeholder-5.jpg)

Pepper soup is a night business. Aunty Bola opens at 7 PM and closes whenever the goat meat runs out — usually around 1 AM. Her two largest cost lines are goat meat and the generator that powers her chest freezer. The freezer matters more than the lights.
